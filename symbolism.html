Symbolism (1880s–1900)

Symbolism developed in the late 19th century as a reaction against realism and materialism. Rather than depicting visible reality, Symbolist artists sought to express inner emotion, spirituality, and dreams.

The movement emphasized atmosphere, metaphor, and psychological depth. Figures often appear suspended in ambiguous spaces, blending decorative composition with mystical content.

Representative Artists

Odilon Redon, The Cyclops, 1898

Gustave Moreau, Jupiter and Semele, 1895

Symbolism influenced Art Nouveau by introducing an emphasis on expressive line and the symbolic female figure. The decorative surface became a space not only for ornament, but for emotional and spiritual projection.

This shift from realism to interiority parallels the transformation of graphic design at the turn of the century. Illustration became more atmospheric, less literal, and more integrated with typography and pattern.
